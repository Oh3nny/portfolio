# About Oheneba (Ohenny) — Personal Biography & Knowledge Base

## Who I Am

My name is Oheneba, though most people who know me call me Ohenny. I'm a designer and creative thinker with a background in architecture and a growing passion for digital product design and technology. My story is one of creativity finding its medium — from drawing on walls as a child, to designing buildings, to now wanting to shape the digital products people interact with every day.

---

## Where It All Started

Creativity has been part of my DNA from as far back as I can remember. My mother studied textiles at university, and her early drawings and work were among the first things that sparked something in me. By the time I was five, I was drawing on any surface I could find the moment I got my hands on a pencil.

Alongside that creativity came an insatiable curiosity about how things worked. I dismantled my toys to understand what was inside them, and when I ran out of toys to take apart, I'd improvise — using the wheels from a toy car and juice cartons to build something entirely new. The people around me, my parents, my teachers, my peers, noticed this quality early. It wasn't just the making — it was the thinking behind the making.

---

## Technology Finds Me Early

At ten years old, in 2011, I spotted an iPhone 3GS on eBay and begged my mum to buy it for me. The moment I held it, I was fascinated — not just by the games like Angry Birds and Temple Run, but by the design, the interface, the way it felt in your hand. It didn't take long before I was deep in YouTube tutorials trying to figure out how to get apps I couldn't afford. That search led me to jailbreaking.

I discovered Cydia, installed mods, and started customising my phone in ways Apple hadn't intended — unique visuals, features the stock OS didn't offer. For a ten-year-old, this was a revelation. From that point on, I kept up with everything happening in tech through YouTube, absorbing it all quietly in the background, year after year.

---

## The Creative Kid in School

Throughout primary school, my creativity was visible to everyone. I'd bring artwork and drawings from home to show classmates, post work on DeviantArt and online art competition sites, and share what I'd made. Teachers noticed, and eventually started giving me a platform — letting me lead art classes, showing other kids how to draw or create, introducing techniques like the 3D hand illusion. Whenever the school needed something creative, I was the one they called on.

That pattern continued into secondary school. I gravitated toward subjects like Maths, Graphic Communication, Design & Manufacturing, and Art & Design. These were the classes where I felt most alive, most capable. But when it came to thinking about a career, my options felt narrow. I knew I wanted something creative with real prospects, and the only path I could clearly see was architecture. I'd never heard of UI/UX design. I didn't know that the apps and software I'd been so drawn to had designers behind them — I assumed engineers built everything. So architecture it was.

---

## Architecture School: The Hard Road to Clarity

I started university studying architecture, and almost immediately I had doubts. I told my dad I wasn't sure it was for me — but with nothing else clearly in mind, I stayed. First year went well enough. Then second year arrived, and it nearly broke me.

It was the height of the pandemic. Lectures were online, communication was fragmented, and without a capable computer, I couldn't express my design ideas the way I could see them in my head. My first proper design critique happened over Zoom, and it was brutal. I considered quitting. But I passed, and that became the motivation to keep going.

For third year, I invested in a proper machine with a solid NVIDIA GPU, and everything shifted. Freed from the technical limitations that had been holding me back, I could finally create without interruption. Third year became my best year in architecture school. My design thinking sharpened. My confidence grew. I carried that momentum through fourth year and graduated with a Bachelor's with Honours — a 2:1.

---

## Masters, AI, and the Pivot

I went on to complete a Master's in Architecture, and for my thesis I explored something that genuinely excited me: the use of generative AI in the architectural design process. I tested a range of text-to-image and image-to-image models, examining how they could be embedded into the design workflow. Bridging technology and design felt natural — it was the first time in architecture school where both sides of me, the creative and the technologically curious, were working together fully.

I finished my thesis in November 2024. In the months that followed, I watched the AI landscape shift dramatically. Models I had tested during my research were already being phased out, replaced by more powerful tools. That velocity made me think hard about the industries I wanted to be part of.

Architecture, for all its rigour and beauty, moves slowly — in process, in tooling, in culture. I wanted something faster. Something where design decisions ship, where contributions are visible, where there's real ownership and constant learning.

---

## The Digital Environment

Over the past year I've been self-teaching UI/UX and product design — using YouTube, exploring tools, and drawing on the design process skills I built throughout architecture school. The parallels are real: both disciplines are about understanding people, defining problems, and constructing solutions with clarity and intent. But the digital environment moves at a pace that architecture simply can't match.

I'm particularly drawn to the intersection of design and technology — the way tools like Claude Code are enabling designers to prototype faster, the way AI is fundamentally changing what's possible in creative workflows. That's the space I want to be in. Not just designing interfaces, but helping shape how people experience technology.

I swapped the built environment for the digital one. And this time, I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be.

## Where I'm From

I live in Glasgow now, and I've lived there the whole time since moving to the UK at seven years old. Before that, I was brought up in Ghana. That mix of cultures has shaped a lot of how I see the world — it gave me one foundation early on, then a completely different environment to grow into after moving.

Being Ghanaian and growing up between those two contexts has given me a strong sense of identity, but also a kind of adaptability. I think it made me observant from early on — noticing differences in people, environment, pace, and culture in a way that sticks with you.

---

## Travel and Perspective

I've been to Japan, Brazil, Dubai, Italy, Switzerland, New York, Ghana, and Paris. Travelling matters to me because it shifts my perspective fast — different places have their own rhythm, visuals, energy, and way of life, and I always come back noticing something new.

It's one of the things that keeps me curious. Whether it's architecture, atmosphere, street life, food, or just how a city feels, I like being in places that make me pay attention.

## Tools and Creative Workflow

I like working across both traditional creative tools and newer AI tools. Some of the tools I use include Photoshop, Illustrator, Figma, Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, DaVinci Resolve, Midjourney, Nano Banana, Gemini, Kling, and other AI video tools.

I've been using Photoshop and Illustrator since high school, and I carried both through my architecture degree too. That whole architecture training really sharpened how I think about layout, presentation, and defending why a design decision makes sense when someone starts poking holes in it.

I do not code in the traditional sense, but I use AI tools to speed up my workflow and prototype ideas quickly. For me, those tools are less about pretending to be an engineer and more about getting ideas out faster, testing directions, and making the creative process more fluid.

I'm especially into using AI for prototyping and creative exploration. I like testing ideas quickly, seeing what sticks, and using different tools depending on whether I'm designing, editing visuals, generating concepts, or building something interactive.

## Outside of Design

Outside of design, I'm into basketball, cinematography, travelling, and spending time with my friends and family. I like having interests that pull me away from screens and work mode a bit — whether that's playing ball, being around good people, or taking in visuals and atmosphere through film and photography.

That side of me matters because it keeps me curious and balanced. A lot of what I notice in everyday life — movement, rhythm, composition, energy, how people interact — ends up feeding back into how I think creatively.
